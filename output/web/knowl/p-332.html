Unlike past changes to the climate, the current change is driven almost entirely by the actions of a single species - ours. As early as the 1820s, the French mathematician and physicist Joseph Fourier realized that the Earth’s atmosphere must trap some of the sun’s warmth - otherwise the Earth would be as cold as the moon [4.11.1.37]. In 1859, the Irish physicist John Tyndall proved in a laboratory setting that certain gases - including water vapor (\(H_2O\)), carbon dioxide (\(CO_2\)), and methane (\(CH_4\)) - were trapping heat in the Earth’s atmosphere. In 1896, a Swedish physicist, Svante Arrhenius, who was studying carbon dioxide levels during the current time and during the last ice age, coined the term greenhouse effect [4.11.1.37].
